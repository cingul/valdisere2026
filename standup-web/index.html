Motivation

The Problem

Orthostatic Hypotension (OH) affects 16-30% of older adults, carrying severe morbidity including increased risk of falls, atrial fibrillation, venous thromboembolism, and chronic kidney disease.

Current pharmacological treatments (Midodrine, Fludrocortisone, Droxidopa) are often inadequate, providing only transient relief while introducing significant side effects such as supine hypertension and heart failure. This therapeutic gap leaves millions vulnerable.

Why It Matters

Orthostatic Hypotension is not just dizziness. It is a significant predictor of mortality.

Data from the influential ARIC Study (Atherosclerosis Risk in Communities) highlights the increased hazard. Even after adjusting for age, sex, and comorbidities, individuals with OH face a nearly doubled risk of all-cause mortality.

Physiological Rationale

Systemic arterial pressure is maintained via a complex orchestration of physical, chemical, and biological processes. We propose that supracardiac venous outflow impairment disrupts this regulation via several interconnected mechanisms.

1. Baroreflex & Metabolite Clearance

The body’s primary defense against OH is the baroreflex. Baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch detect pressure changes, transmitting signals to the NTS in the brainstem. The medulla then modulates sympathetic outflow to adjust heart rate and vascular tone.

Efficiency depends on proper metabolite clearance (Fick principle), where removal is proportional to blood flow. Impaired venous outflow disrupts this coordination, exacerbating OH.

Slow Feedback Responder

The baroreflex acts as a reactive feedback loop with a latency of 2–3 seconds, correcting pressure only after a drop is detected.

2. Venous Return Dynamics

Venous return (VR) is defined as the difference between mean systemic filling pressure (Pmsf) and right atrial pressure (RAP), divided by resistance to venous return (RVR):

VR = Pmsf - RAP / RVR

Gravity causes pooling of >500ml of blood in the upright position. Standard physiological compensation fails when venous valvular stenoses impede flow, directly increasing RVR and decreasing cardiac output by nearly 20%.

3. Vestibulo-Sympathetic Reflex

This reflex integrates vestibular inputs to anticipate postural changes. In OH patients, venous congestion leads to poor metabolite clearance in the rostral ventrolateral medulla.

This causes "Sympathetic Anticipation Failure"—an inability to activate vasoconstriction prior to standing, resulting in exaggerated blood pressure drops.

Rapid Feed-Forward Responder

The VSR is an anticipatory "feed-forward" system, activating within <1 second (<100ms) upon head movement to prevent drops before they occur.

4. Static Venous Tone

We suspect venous tone in OH patients becomes static, failing to adjust dynamically. This creates a paradox: excessive constriction in supine (Supine Hypertension) and inadequate constriction in standing (Orthostatic Hypotension).

Alleviating obstruction may restore dynamic tone, treating biomechanical failure at its source.

5. Glymphatic Clearance

The glymphatic system clears metabolic waste along perivenous spaces. Venous engorgement constricts these spaces, impeding drainage.

According to the Fick principle, slowed microcirculation reduces metabolite removal from critical brainstem centers. Restoring outflow via stenting re-sensitizes these autonomic centers.

The Intervention

Venous Stenting

Venous stenting aims to address the flow-dependent mechanisms outlined in the Physiological Rationale: Baroreflex latency, Venous Return, and Glymphatic Clearance.

Procedural Technique

9F sheath left femoral vein access. TracStar to right jugular vein. Pressure measurement. IVUS of superior sagittal sinus to SVC. Venography. Stent Abre based on imaging. Post-stenting pressure measurements and venography. TracStar to left jugular vein. Pressure measurements. IVUS left transverse sinus to SVC. Venography and Abre stent based on measurements. Post-stenting pressure measurements and venography.

Patient Profile

Clinical History

Ischemic Cardiomyopathy & Migraines: History of ischemic cardiomyopathy following DHE treatment for severe migraines (2003) and chronic left ventricle thrombus requiring warfarin therapy. Notable history of interstitial lung disease and myocardial infarction (2001).

Symptom Onset & Progression: The patient first experienced syncope in January 2025. These episodes recurred repeatedly until August 2025, when the phenotype transitioned to a violent orthostatic tremor.

Recent Events: On 1/31/25, she experienced a syncopal episode in a bank, striking her right side and sustaining a spinal fracture. She continues to report 10/10 back pain. Similar episodes occurred the previous summer in Cape May and during apple picking in the fall.

Current Presentation: This 79-year-old female is now severely symptomatic and functionally debilitated. Standing still for even a few seconds induces a violent tremor, preventing basic activities of daily living such as showering, cooking, or shopping.

Key Symptoms

  • Headaches and "echo" sensation in ears (Pulsatile Tinnitus)
